The Problem in Dollar Terms

Here's the math most revenue cycle leaders don't want to see: your collectors are almost certainly spending significant time on accounts they'll never collect, while high-probability accounts age in the queue.

The typical healthcare organization with $50M+ in annual patient revenue has somewhere between $2-4M sitting in suboptimally prioritized collection queues at any given time. Not uncollectableâ€”just being worked in the wrong order.

The standard approachâ€”FIFO queues, broad payer buckets, or static rules based on balance thresholdsâ€”treats all $10,000 accounts the same regardless of whether they're 90% likely to pay or 10%. Your best collectors waste hours on accounts that were never going to pay, while accounts that would have paid with one phone call age into write-off territory.

The industry averages: 45-day revenue cycles, 12% denial rates, and significant staff time spent on claims that would have resolved anyway. Organizations that fix this see cycles compress to 28 days and denial write-offs drop materiallyâ€”often 200-300 basis points of EBITDA improvement.

What this looks like in practice:

A scoring model assigns a "Likelihood to Collect" score to every outstanding accountâ€”updated dailyâ€”based on payment history, payer type, aging patterns, and contact outcomes. Instead of working accounts by age or balance, collectors start each day with a queue optimized for actual collection probability. The result: same team, same hours, more cash collected.

The underlying pattern is one we've applied in clinical contexts: our MDS Optimization platform uses similar scoring logic to prioritize which assessments need attention, surfacing the highest-value opportunities for MDS coordinators. The technical architectureâ€”ingesting historical data, training a model on your outcomes, delivering prioritized recommendations into daily workflowâ€”translates directly to revenue cycle applications.

The 5-Minute Fit Assessment

Answer these honestly. If you check 4 or more, this is worth exploring. If you check fewer than 3, you likely have bigger fish to fry first.

  • You have 18+ months of claims history with outcomes
    The model needs to learn what "success" looks like at your organization. If your billing data is fragmented across systems or you've changed practice management platforms recently, that's a hurdle (not a dealbreaker, but a hurdle).
  • Your collectors work a queue they didn't design

What You Need to Have Ready

This isn't a technical checklistâ€”it's about organizational readiness.

Data access (straightforward)

You'll need to export historical claims data with payment outcomes. Most billing systems can do this. The exact fields vary by system, but generally: account balances, aging, payer information, contact history, and payment/write-off outcomes.

No EHR integration required. No clinical data. This is purely a billing/collections play.

A baseline you can measure against

Before you start, document your current state: bad debt expense as % of revenue, average days in A/R, cash collected per FTE per day (or month). You can't prove ROI without a clear "before."

Process change authority

The person sponsoring this project needs the authority to actually change how collectors work. If the recommendation is "work account X before account Y," someone needs to make that happen.

Realistic timeline expectations

Plan for 8-12 weeks to deployment, then 60-90 days of parallel running before you'll have confident data on performance. This isn't a quick fixâ€”it's a capability that compounds over time as the model learns your patterns.

Internal communication

Your collectors will have questions. "Why is the computer telling me to call this account?" Prepare them for the change, explain the logic, and create a feedback loop so the model can learn from their expertise.

The Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Decision

Let's be direct about when each path makes sense.

Build internally when:

  • You have a data science team with healthcare revenue cycle experience (rare)
  • You're willing to accept 6-12 month timelines and iteration
  • You have executive patience for a project that won't show ROI for a year
  • You want full control and are willing to maintain it indefinitely

Buy off-the-shelf when:

  • Your problem is generic enough that industry averages work
  • You're okay with limited customization
  • You want predictable subscription costs
  • You don't expect the tool to learn your specific patterns

Partner for custom when:

  • Your collection patterns are organizationally specific
  • You want a system that learns from your outcomes over time
  • You need accountability to results, not just software delivery
  • You're willing to invest more upfront for higher ROI

Reality check:

MIT's research found internal AI builds succeed about 33% of the time. The failures usually aren't technicalâ€”they're organizational. The team builds something that works in testing, can't get it integrated into workflows, and it dies.

The limitation of off-the-shelf: these tools train on industry data, not your data. The patient demographics that predict payment at a large urban health system may predict default at a rural critical access hospital. You're getting average insights for your above-average problems.

The tradeoff for custom solutions is cost and timeline. Custom solutions require discovery, development, and iteration. They take longer and cost more than plugging in a vendor tool. The MIT research found they also reach production twice as often and deliver results that generic tools miss.

The honest middle ground:

For many organizations, the right answer is to start with a simpler versionâ€”even rules-based prioritization tuned to your dataâ€”and graduate to ML-based scoring once you've proven the process change works. Don't over-engineer the first step.

Red Flags: When to Wait

Sometimes the right answer is "not yet." Here's when:

Your billing data is unreliable

If your team doesn't trust the data in your practice management systemâ€”duplicate accounts, missing outcomes, inconsistent codingâ€”fix that first. Garbage in, garbage out.

  • You're mid-system migration
    If you're switching billing platforms in the next 12 months, wait. The model will need to be retrained on the new system anyway, and you'll have bigger problems during migration.
  • Collections isn't actually your problem
    If your issue is denials (claims never getting paid), not collections (getting paid on clean claims), this isn't your highest-value project. Denial prediction is a different tool.
  • No one will change the process
    If your revenue cycle team is resistant to changing workflows, or leadership won't mandate the change, save your money. The model only works if people use it.
  • You need results in 30 days
    This isn't a quick fix. If you're in a cash crisis and need immediate results, you need a different interventionâ€”probably temporary staff or outsourcing, not AI.
