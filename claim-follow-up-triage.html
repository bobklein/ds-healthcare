The Problem in Dollar Terms

Your follow-up team is probably working claims in the wrong order.

Most revenue cycle operations work claims by payer queue, aging bucket, or simple FIFO logic. The implicit assumption: all 30-day claims are equally urgent, all Blue Cross claims deserve the same attention, all $5,000 balances should be prioritized the same way.

That assumption costs you money every day.

Some claims that look routine will become problems—they'll age past timely filing limits, require multiple appeals, or end up written off. Others that look complicated will resolve themselves. Your follow-up team can't tell the difference until it's often too late.

The industry averages: 45-day revenue cycles, 12% denial rates, and significant staff time spent on claims that would have resolved anyway. Organizations that fix this see cycles compress to 28 days and denial write-offs drop materially—often 200-300 basis points of EBITDA improvement.

What predictive claim triage actually does:

A scoring engine compares each active claim to your historical patterns—claims that aged out, claims that required multiple touches, claims that denied unexpectedly—and assigns a dynamic risk score. High-risk claims surface immediately. Low-risk claims wait. The result: your best people work the claims that actually need intervention, while self-resolving claims don't consume their time.

This is the same pattern we've applied in clinical risk scoring. Our RAF Score work for a value-based care provider serving 20K+ patients across 7 states uses similar logic—ingesting data from multiple sources, identifying risk patterns, and surfacing prioritized recommendations for clinician action. The technical architecture translates directly: instead of identifying patients at risk of hospitalization, you're identifying claims at risk of write-off.

The 5-Minute Fit Assessment

This isn't right for everyone. Check the boxes that apply:

  • You have significant claim volume (10,000+ claims/month)
    The model needs pattern recognition across thousands of claims to identify meaningful risk signals. Lower volumes can still benefit, but the ROI math changes.
  • Your follow-up team spends time on claims that resolve without intervention
    If you've measured this and found that 20-30% of worked claims would have paid anyway, there's room to redirect that effort.
  • You have claims aging past timely filing limits or appeal windows
    This is the expensive failure mode. If your team consistently misses deadlines on workable claims, prioritization is part of the problem.
  • Your denial patterns vary by payer, service line, or facility
    Generic rules don't capture organizational nuance. If your denial patterns are different at different locations or with different payers, custom scoring has an advantage.
  • You have 2+ years of claims history with outcomes
    The model learns from your past. More history = better pattern recognition. If you've recently changed billing systems, that's a complication.
  • Someone is accountable for A/R days and write-off rates
    There needs to be an owner who cares about these metrics and has authority to change workflows.

If you checked 4 or more, this is worth serious consideration. Fewer than 3, and you likely have higher-priority problems to solve first.

What You Need to Have Ready

Data requirements (simpler than you might think)

This solution sits entirely in your billing workflow. You need:

  • Historical claims data with submission dates and outcomes
  • Payer identification
  • Service/procedure information
  • Aging and status history
  • Denial reason codes where applicable

You don't need EHR integration. You don't need clinical documentation. You don't need payer contract details (that's a different solution).

The baseline measurement challenge

Proving ROI requires knowing your "before" state:

  • Average days in A/R by payer category
  • Write-off rate for claims that were worked vs. not worked
  • Staff hours per claim by category
  • Timely filing misses per month

If you don't have these numbers, getting them is step one—before you invest in any solution.

Process change readiness

The technology is the easy part. The hard part: convincing your follow-up team to trust a score they didn't create. You'll need:

  • Clear executive sponsorship
  • Training on the "why" behind the scores
  • A feedback mechanism when the model is wrong
  • Patience during the learning curve (first 30-60 days will feel uncomfortable)

Timeline expectations

Plan for 8-12 weeks from kickoff to go-live, then 60-90 days of measurement before you'll have statistically confident results. The model improves over time as it learns your patterns, so results at month 6 are typically better than month 3.

The Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Decision

Build internally if:

  • You have data science resources with healthcare revenue cycle experience
  • Your IT team has capacity for a 6-12 month project
  • You're comfortable with ongoing model maintenance and retraining
  • You want complete control of the intellectual property

Buy off-the-shelf if:

  • Your claim patterns are similar to the vendor's training data
  • You're okay with limited customization to your specific patterns
  • You want predictable monthly costs
  • Speed to deployment matters more than optimization

Partner for custom if:

  • Your claim patterns are organizationally specific
  • You want a model that learns from your outcomes over time
  • You need accountability to results, not just software metrics
  • You're willing to invest more for higher accuracy

Reality check:

MIT's research found internal AI builds fail 67% of the time. The failures aren't usually technical—they're organizational. The model works in testing, but never gets adopted by the team.

The limitation of off-the-shelf tools: they train on industry data. Your hospital's claim patterns—shaped by your specific payer mix, coding practices, and operational quirks—may not match the averages.

What makes the difference? In our experience, it's workflow integration and change management—not model sophistication. Our Never Alone platform for CommuniCare went from MVP to 7-state deployment in 18 months because we designed it with end users from day one and embedded it into their existing workflows. The same principle applies to revenue cycle: a brilliant model that sits outside your team's daily process won't get used.

Practical starting point:

For many organizations, the honest first step is improving your rules-based logic with better data analysis before investing in ML. Analyze your historical write-offs—what patterns emerge? Sometimes a few targeted rule changes get you 60% of the benefit at 10% of the cost.

Red Flags: When to Wait

  • Your claim data is fragmented across systems
    If claims live in multiple systems that don't talk to each other, consolidation comes before optimization.
  • You're about to change billing platforms
    Model training on your current system will be wasted if you're migrating in 12 months. Wait until post-migration stabilization.
  • Denials are your primary problem, not follow-up prioritization
    If your issue is claims getting denied in the first place (not follow-up on pending claims), you need denial prediction and pre-submission scrubbing—a different solution.
  • Staff won't trust the output
    If your follow-up team has deep resistance to changing their workflow, or management won't mandate the change, the technology won't matter. Solve the people problem first.
  • You need results this quarter
    This is a capability that builds over time. If you're in a cash crisis, you need faster interventions—staffing, outsourcing, focused payment plan pushes—not AI implementation.

Questions to Ask Any Vendor

On their methodology:

  • How does your model differentiate high-risk claims from low-risk? What signals matter most?"
  • Is the model trained on industry data or our data specifically?"
  • How do you handle claims that don't match patterns the model has seen?"
  • What's the false positive rate? How often will it flag claims that don't actually need intervention?"

On implementation:

  • What does this look like in my team's daily workflow? New system or integration with existing tools?"
  • Who handles change management and training? What's included vs. additional cost?"
  • What's your track record getting solutions from pilot to production?"

On results:

  • What specific metrics should we expect to improve, and by how much?"
  • How long until we see statistically significant results?"
  • Show me a comparable client where you improved A/R days or write-off rates—can I talk to them?"
  • Are you willing to tie your fees to outcome metrics?"

On sustainability:

  • Does the model degrade over time without retraining? What's the maintenance requirement?"
  • If our payer mix or claim patterns change, how does the model adapt?"
  • What do we own vs. what do you own? What happens if we end the relationship?"

Vendors with vague answers to these questions—especially on accountability and references—should give you pause.
