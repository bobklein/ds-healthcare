The Problem in Dollar Terms

You're almost certainly being underpaid on some percentage of your claims—and you don't know which ones.

Payer contracts are dense, multi-hundred-page documents with carve-outs, fee schedule escalators, service-specific provisions, and amendment layers accumulated over years of renegotiation. No human can hold all of that complexity in their head while reviewing thousands of remittances per week.

The result: systematic underpayment. Not fraud—just payers applying their interpretation of ambiguous language, and providers lacking the bandwidth to catch every discrepancy.

Most healthcare organizations leave 1-3% of net revenue on the table due to contract underpayment they never identify. For an organization with $50M in net patient revenue, that's $500K-$1.5M annually—not in new revenue, but in revenue you've already earned and simply aren't collecting.

What AI-powered contract interpretation does:

A system ingests your complete payer contract library—PDFs, amendments, fee schedules—and builds a structured understanding of what you should be paid for each service. It then compares every remittance against those contractual terms and flags claims where payment appears below allowable, citing the specific contract language. No more spot-checking. No more "we think they might be underpaying." Systematic identification with documentation ready for appeal.

The document intelligence challenge here is similar to what we solved in our MDS Optimization work—where NLP scans provider notes and progress reports to surface coding opportunities and flag inconsistencies. The difference is the source material (contracts vs. clinical documentation) and the output (underpayment flags vs. assessment recommendations), but the underlying capability is the same: extracting structured, actionable intelligence from complex healthcare documents.

The 5-Minute Fit Assessment

This solution isn't right for everyone. Check what applies:

  • You have multiple payer contracts with different terms
    If you have one dominant payer with straightforward fee schedules, manual auditing may suffice. The complexity advantage emerges with 5+ contracts, each with unique provisions.
  • Your contracts include provisions beyond simple fee schedules
    Carve-outs for specific services, escalators tied to volume or quality metrics, outlier provisions, case rate calculations—this is where underpayment hides.
  • You don't have dedicated staff auditing every remittance against contracts
    If you're spot-checking 10% of remittances or only auditing when something looks wrong, you're missing underpayment on the other 90%.
  • Your contract management is document-based, not structured data
    If your contracts live in PDFs and Word documents rather than a centralized system that maps payments to terms, there's no way to audit systematically.
  • You've had successful payment appeals in the past
    If you've won appeals on underpayment issues, that confirms the problem exists. The question is how much you're missing that you never identified.
  • Someone owns contract performance
    You need an internal champion—managed care director, CFO, revenue cycle VP—who cares about contractual reimbursement accuracy and will act on findings.

If you checked 4 or more, this is likely high-value. Fewer than 3 suggests you may have simpler contract structures where manual processes work.

What You Need to Have Ready

Your contract library (the raw material)

This solution depends on having your actual contracts—the documents that specify what you're owed. That includes:

  • Base contracts for each payer
  • All amendments and addendums
  • Current fee schedules
  • Any side letters or negotiated exceptions

Many organizations discover their contract library is incomplete. Finding those missing documents is often step one.

Remittance data (the comparison point)

You need electronic remittance data (835s) or detailed payment records that can be matched to contract terms. The more history, the more potential recovery. Most implementations start with 6-12 months of remittance data.

No EHR integration required

This is a billing/finance solution. You don't need clinical data. You don't need EHR access. The data sources are contracts (documents) and payments (835s/remittances).

Realistic expectations on timeline

Contract ingestion takes time. A typical implementation: 4-6 weeks to process contracts and build the knowledge base, then ongoing comparison against new remittances. Initial findings often surface in weeks, but comprehensive results take 2-3 months.

Appeal and recovery capacity

Identifying underpayment is only valuable if you pursue recovery. You need staff (or a partner) who will file appeals, negotiate with payers, and follow through. Some organizations use contract interpretation findings to inform renegotiation rather than individual appeals.

The Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Decision

Build internally if:

  • You have NLP/document AI expertise in-house
  • Your contracts are relatively standardized
  • You're willing to maintain and update the system as contracts change
  • You want full control over the methodology

Buy off-the-shelf if:

  • Your contracts are similar to the vendor's existing knowledge base
  • You're comfortable with their interpretation methodology
  • You want predictable subscription costs
  • You can accept some false positives

Partner for custom if:

  • Your contracts include unusual or highly negotiated provisions
  • You want high-precision identification (fewer false positives)
  • You need help with recovery, not just identification
  • You want the system to learn from your appeals outcomes

Reality check:

Contract interpretation is a hard NLP problem. The nuance in healthcare payer contracts—nested conditions, cross-referenced fee schedules, effective date complexities—defeats most general-purpose document AI. Building this well takes specialized expertise.

The limitation of off-the-shelf: tools that haven't seen your specific contracts may misinterpret unique provisions. Your negotiated terms are, by definition, different from industry standard.

The investment for custom is higher. The precision is also higher. And unlike claims prioritization, this is a domain where your proprietary data—your actual contracts—is genuinely unique. No vendor has trained on your negotiated terms.

Our work with CommuniCare Advantage involved a similar challenge: making sense of a complex ecosystem. They had 60+ digital products, fragmented data sources, and no systematic way to understand what was happening across their operation. Hybrid approach:

Many organizations start with a sampling exercise: manually audit 100 randomly selected remittances against contract terms. If you find underpayment in 2-5% of claims, the math supports investment. If you find negligible variance, your payers may actually be paying correctly.

Red Flags: When to Wait

  • Your contract library is incomplete or disorganized
    If you can't locate current contracts for your major payers, fix that first. The AI can only interpret contracts you can provide.
  • You're mid-contract renegotiation
    If major payer contracts are being renegotiated in the next 6 months, wait. The system will need to re-learn the new terms anyway.
  • You have no recovery capacity
    Identifying underpayment without pursuing recovery is an expensive exercise in frustration. Make sure you have staff or partners who will act on findings.
  • Your major payers use standard rates with minimal negotiation
    If your top payers are Medicare/Medicaid with standard fee schedules, there's less room for interpretation variance. The complexity advantage emerges with commercially negotiated contracts.
  • You're in a cash crisis
    Contract recovery takes time—identifying underpayment, filing appeals, waiting for responses. If you need cash in 30 days, this isn't the lever. This is a 6-12 month play.

Questions to Ask Any Vendor

On methodology:

  • How do you handle contract ambiguity? When contract language could be interpreted multiple ways, how does your system decide?"
  • What's your false positive rate? What percentage of flagged underpayments turn out to be correct payments on investigation?"
  • How do you handle amendments and fee schedule updates? Does someone need to manually input changes?"
  • Show me how you interpret a complex provision—walk me through your reasoning."

On your specific contracts:

  • Have you worked with contracts from [our major payers] before?"
  • What happens when you encounter provisions you haven't seen?"
  • Can I see example interpretations from contracts similar to ours?"

On implementation:

  • What format do you need contracts in? Can you handle scanned PDFs or only text-searchable?"
  • How long until we see first findings? How long until comprehensive results?"
  • What does the output look like? A list of underpaid claims? Documentation for appeals?"

On results:

  • What's the typical recovery rate for organizations similar to ours?"
  • Can you share a reference where you identified $X in underpayment? Can I call them?"
  • Do you help with appeals, or just identification?"
  • Are you willing to tie compensation to recovery, not just identification?"

On sustainability:

  • When contracts change, what's the process to update the system?"
  • Who owns the contract interpretations you develop—us or you?"
  • What happens if a payer disputes your interpretation?"
